Question

Deep in the wilderness, near Chalk River, a lumberjack encounters an irradiated poplar tree that has grown into a tall and perfect cone. He falls the tree, but as soon as it hits the ground, two ravenous mutant beavers dart out of the forest and start devouring it from opposite ends. The helpless lumberjack can do nothing but watch. After the tree has been eaten, he observes that the beaver, which was eating from the thin end, ate \(1/3\) of the length of the tree. How much of the tree has he eaten by weight, if the total mass of thre tree was \(270\text{ kg}\)? Answer in kg.
\(\textbf{(A) } 9 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 10 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 30 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 60 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 90 \)


Solution

The volume of the entire tree is: $$V_\text{total} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2h$$ Using similar triangles, if the length of a cone is scaled by a factor \(x\) then the radius is also scaled by the same factor. Thus, the volume of the tree that the beaver ate can be written as: \begin{align*} V_\text{eaten} &= \frac{1}{3}\pi\left(\frac{r}{3}\right)^2\left(\frac{h}{3}\right) \\ &= \frac{1}{27}\left(\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2h\right) \\ &= \frac{1}{27}V_{total} \end{align*} Mass is proportional to volume. Thus, the mass the beaver ate is \(\frac{1}{27}\) of the total mass which is equal to \(270\cdot\frac{1}{27}=\boxed{\textbf{(B) }10}\)

Remarks

  • Even if you forgot the exact formula for the volume of a cone but you know that \(V_\text{cone} \propto r^2h\), you can still derive the correct answer.
