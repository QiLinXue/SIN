Question

For his float entry for the Oktoberfest parade,Onkel Hans builds a giant keg and fills it up with delicious draft beer. The keg is a cylinder of radius \(R=5\text{ m}\), and sits on a large carousel platform that spins about its central vertical axis. An inebriated baton twirler marching enxt to the float observes that, in its steady state, the surface of the beer is curved as a parabola. It is shallowest at the center of the keg and deepest at the edge. What must be the frequency of rotation be so that this difference in depth equals the radius of the keg? Answer in rad/sec. Hint: the slope of the function \(h(r)=r^2\) is \(2r\).
\(\textbf{(A) } 0.099 \qquad\textbf{(B) } 0.315 \qquad\textbf{(C) } 0.560 \qquad\textbf{(D) } 0.625 \qquad\textbf{(E) } 1.98 \)


Solution 1

First, determine the equation of the parabola. Let the vertex be centered at the origin and we have \(h(5)=5=k(5)^2\). Solving gives \(k=\frac{1}{5}\) such that the equation of the curve is represented by \(h(r)=\frac{1}{5}r^2\)

In the free body diagram shown below, we draw the forces for an arbitrary water molecule at a horizontal distance \(r=a\) away from the origin. We wish to sum up the forces in the vertical and horizontal components, but first we need to determine \(\theta\).

Since the slope at \(r=a\) is \(\frac{2}{5}a\), the slope of the perpendicular normal force is \(\frac{-1}{0.4a}\) and therefore: $$\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{0.4a}\right)$$ Summing up the forces in the y- and x- direction gives: \begin{align*} N\sin\left(\frac{1}{0.4a}\right) &= mg & (1)\\ N\cos\left(\frac{1}{0.4a}\right) &= m\omega^2 a & (2) \end{align*} Taking the ratio \(\frac{(1)}{(2)}\) gives: \begin{align*} \tan\left(\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{0.4a}\right)\right) &= \frac{mg}{m\omega^2 a} \\ \frac{1}{0.4a} &= \frac{g}{\omega^2 a} \\ \omega &= \sqrt{0.4(9.8)} \\ \end{align*} Evaluating gives \(\boxed{\textbf{(E) } 1.98}\)

Solution 2

Okay get ready for an epic solution. We shall introduce the idea of the effective potential here. In short, every stationary water surface is a an equipotential surface. This means that if you place a single water molecule at the surface, it takes zero work to move it across the surface. But wait! Water isn't stationary here, it's moving! Well if we actually introduce a fictitious force known as the centrifugal force, we can represent the surface as stationary. The centrifugal force stores a certain potential: $$V = -\int m\omega^2 r dr = - \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2r^2$$ Notice how this is the negative of the kinetic energy. Conservation of energy tells us: $$0 = mgR - \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2R^2$$ where the potential energy of the bottom of the parabola is set to be zero. Solving for \(\omega\) will yield \(\boxed{\textbf{(E) } 1.98}\).

Remarks

  • Why exactly does the water make a parabolic shape? It can be shown (with quite some work) that a loose hanging chain actually takes the shape of a hyperbolic cosine curve so why should fluids be any different? In fact, by looking at pressure variations it can be shown that the surface of the water in a rotating cylinder follows the curve \(y=\frac{\omega^2r^2}{2g}\) See Physics 5th edition by HRK pg 351 for more details on how to approach this problem.
